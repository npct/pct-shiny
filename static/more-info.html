The Propensity to Cycle Tool (PCT) was designed to assist transport planners and policy makers to priorities investments and interventions to promote cycling. The key question that the PCT answers is "where is cycling currently common and where does cycling have the greatest potential to grow?"

By answering this question, the PCT can be used to decide along which routes new cycle paths should be built. By helping to identify bottle necks in the system (e.g. unexpectedly low levels of cycling between two areas due to a physical barrier to cycling such as a major road), the PCT can also help decide where smaller pieces of infrastructure should go.

The PCT is primarily a strategic planning tool. It is intended to encourage users to think on larger time scales and geographic scales. The temporal element of the tool is included through the various of scenarios of change, ranging from the government's target to double cycling in a decade to the more ambitious 'Go Dutch' scenario whereby Dutch levels of cycling are reached in England.

The PCT can therefore be used as a 'visioning' tool as well as a narrowly fosused transport planning aid. By showing what the rate of cycling could feasible look like in different parts of cities and regions, and illustrating the associated increase in cycle use on the road network, the PCT should inform policies that seek a wider shift towards sustainable transport more generally. In this context the PCT can be used, for example, to decide where to re-allocate space away from cars and towards cycling. The Satellite view (described below) can help with this, by showing how many lanes of the road are currently allocated to cars. Wider policies that the PCT could help inform include where to install new speed bumps, speed cameras, 20 mph speed limits and where to target residents with pro-cycling policies such as free cycle training.

In summary the PCT is a planning support system to improve cycling provision at many levels from regions to specific points on the road network.
