Key information for the West Midlands, commute layer, MSOA geography

This document provides information about the data underlying the Propensity to Cycle Tool (PCT) commuting layer for the West Midlands. The data were generated on 2019-11-06 and this document was created on 2019-11-06. The PCT is an open source tool for sustainable transport planning, released under the conditions of the Affero GPL. The PCT source code can be modified by others as long as attribution is made to the original.

This layer of the PCT uses origin-destination data on travel to work from the 2011 Census. The interface is currently displaying the number of commuters travelling by different modes between Middle Super Output Area zones (MSOAs, average size 3,325 commuters). There were 935,511 commuters living in the West Midlands recorded in the 2011 Census. All of these commuters are included in the zones data that are visualised on the map interface and available for download.

Adding in inter-regional flows, there were 1,025,774 commuters living or working in the West Midlands in the 2011 Census. All these commuters are included in the All flows files on the data download pages.

Straight lines, Fast routes and Quieter routes are both visualised on the map interface and available to download. In the downloads, these lines and routes contain all 16,471 flows between MSOAs that a) start or end in the West Midlands, b) have a fast route distance <20km, and c) contain at least 10 commuters (by any mode, in either direction). These 16,471 between-zone MSOA flows contain 671,475 commuters who either live or work in the West Midlands. To speed up load times, the online map only shows those line and routes with a fast route distance <10km.

Between-zone MSOA flows exclude within-MSOA travel, when the MSOA zone of origin is the same as the zone of destination. Within-MSOA travel is represented by red points on the map when the lines are shown, and accounts for 50,744 commuters living in the West Midlands. Between-zone flows also exclude those with no fixed place of work.

Finally, the Route Network (LSOA, clickable) and the Route Network (LSOA, image) are built from all between-zone LSOA fast routes (including any that start or end in the West Midlands). In the online version of the clickable route network we only present route segments that contain at least 30 cyclists in the Go Dutch scenario. The downloads include all route segments. For more details please see User Manual C1.

See Lovelace et al. (2017) for details of the methods used to estimate the cycling, health and carbon impacts of each scenario, and the methods used to visualise results at the area, line, route and route network level.


The West Midlands region

Figure 1 shows a map of the West Midlands, including the regional boundary (thick black border), local authority boundaries (blue lines) and MSOA zones in the region (grey with a black border). The straight green lines represent the top 100 flows in terms of the number of commuters by any mode.

Figure 1: Map of the West Midlands region The left-hand map in Figure 2 shows the proportion of commuters in each zone with a fast route commute distance less than 10km (calculated excluding people with no fixed workplace). The average proportion was 66% across zones in the West Midlands, compared with a national average of 56%. The right-hand map shows the average hilliness of the fastest routes used by commuters living in each zone. The average was 2.0% across zones in the West Midlands, compared with a national average of 1.9%.

Cycling levels in each scenario for the West Midlands

At baseline (2011 Census), the proportion of commuters who cycled to work in the West Midlands was 1.7%, compared to the national average of 3.1% in England and Wales as a whole. The percentage of cycling expected was 3%, based on the distance and hilliness of commutes in the region using the baseline model for England and Wales.

Table 1 summarises the proportion of commuters cycling, walking, driving and using other modes under each scenario in the West Midlands, based on aggregating across all residents in all the MSOA zones.

Health and carbon impacts in the West Midlands

A modified version of the 2014 HEAT tool was used to calculate health impacts, based on the extra physical activity gained through new cycling trips in each scenario, minus the physical activity displaced in any former walking trips that have now been switched to cycling. CO2 impacts are derived from changes in distance driven per scenario.

Table 2 illustrates the health and carbon impacts of the different scenarios in the West Midlands, based on aggregating across all residents in all the MSOA zones. For each scenario, comparisons are made with a counterfactual in which nobody in the region cycled ('No cyclists'), and with the observed level of cycling in the 2011 Census.
